A heritage window with white shutters left ajar floats in a sky full of clouds — no wall, no façade around it, just blue and white as far as the eye can see. This painting was born from a pairing exercise between painters and photographers from the Boisbriand Photo Club, during the 2017 edition of "Déclic du pinceau," based on a photograph by Marc-André Thibodeau titled Fenêtre de Normandie. Benoît shifted the viewpoint and heightened the perspective — an exterior window looking out onto the exterior, a deliberate nod to René Magritte. A window opened onto a dream: sky is no limit.

Viollet-le-Duc Wave

36 x 48 po.
Acrylique sur toile galerie
2 350 $

Une vague atlantique peinte d'instinct vient percuter un motif ornemental directement inspiré des relevés architecturaux d'Eugène Viollet-le-Duc — rouge profond, bleu ardoise et or se répétant en un réseau géométrique presque hypnotique. Deux univers qui n'auraient jamais dû se rencontrer, la rigueur du dessin patrimonial et la fureur indisciplinée de l'écume blanche, entrent ici en collision. Ce n'est pas une copie des maîtres : c'est un dialogue, où la structure contient le mouvement sans jamais l'étouffer.

An instinctively painted Atlantic wave crashes into an ornamental motif drawn directly from Eugène Viollet-le-Duc's architectural surveys — deep red, slate blue, and gold repeating in an almost hypnotic geometric network. Two worlds that should never have met — the rigour of heritage drawing and the untamed fury of white foam — collide here. This is not a copy of the masters: it is a dialogue, where structure contains movement without ever smothering it.

The Notre-Dame-de-Bon-Secours chapel, the oldest stone chapel in Montréal, was commissioned by Marguerite Bourgeoys — Ville-Marie's first educator and founder of the Congrégation de Notre-Dame — in 1655. This painting captures the full charm of Old Montréal: the colourful cobblestones of rue Bonsecours and rue Saint-Paul, the bell tower watching over a summer sky crossed by swallows, and the restaurant Les Filles du Roy in the foreground, grounded in the real life of this historic quarter.

Pratto

Acrylique sur toile galerie
2 350 $

Pratto, l'ancien nom du Rocher Percé en Gaspésie, à l'époque où la formation rocheuse de 475 mètres comptait deux arches. Le nom vient de Cap Pratto — le cap du pré, en espagnol — donné par les pêcheurs basques qui fréquentaient ces eaux bien avant l'arrivée de Jacques Cartier. Ce point de vue précis n'est accessible que par bateau : le turquoise profond de l'eau, les ocres et les mauves de la roche composent un réalisme immersif et saisissant.

Pratto, the former name of Percé Rock in the Gaspé, from a time when the 475-metre rock formation still had two arches. The name comes from Cap Pratto — cape of the meadow, in Spanish — given by the Basque fishermen who worked these waters long before Jacques Cartier's arrival. This precise vantage point is only reachable by boat: the deep turquoise of the water, the ochres and mauves of the rock, compose an immersive and striking realism.

The Morgan Arboretum, a 245-hectare forest reserve at the western tip of the island of Montréal, in Sainte-Anne-de-Bellevue, has been part of McGill University's Macdonald Campus since 1945 — a beloved local institution for nature education and outdoor recreation. In this painting, a tall, deep-green conifer stands straight and commanding before a small cottage half-hidden by foliage. The tight vertical composition gives the tree an almost architectural presence, a living pillar as structural as a column.
